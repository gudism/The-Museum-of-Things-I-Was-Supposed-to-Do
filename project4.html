LA Contracts Bot (Prototype)

What it was supposed to be

A small bot that could scan public contract data from Los Angeles and flag anything that looked unusual. Not a full investigation, just a quiet background process that noticed patterns the city preferred to ignore. Los Angeles has a long record of treating corruption as a civic rhythm. The council has an equally long record of participating in scandals with city contractors. The Huizar case was only one example. The goal was to make a system that could watch the details without getting pulled into the noise.

What exists instead

One official document. A collection of fifty public records requests sent to the City Council and to the Office of the City Controller. A paper trail of unanswered emails, broken portals, and delayed responses. A set of scripts that almost run. A folder called data_raw that never filled the way it was supposed to. A five year crashout that became part of the project whether I wanted it or not.

python run_anomaly_detector.py \ --input data_raw/contracts_sample.csv \ --output reports/anomalies.csv

The command looks convincing. The data does not exist yet.

Why it stalled

  • The city transparency infrastructure is built to slow you down.
  • Public records requests took more energy than the actual code.
  • The bureaucracy had more stamina than I did.
  • The story kept expanding and the data never arrived at the pace the project needed.

What this fragment reveals

That corruption in Los Angeles is not a secret or an exception. It is a structural habit that shapes how information moves. That building a bot to track anomalies requires the very data the city makes difficult to access. That sometimes a project exposes its subject by failing. The museum keeps this prototype because the failure is part of the truth. It documents an attempt to follow the money in a place where the pathways are designed to blur. It also shows the limits of one person trying to build clarity inside a system that is not interested in being seen.
