Bukowskis Car (Audio Draft)

What it was supposed to be

A full installation built around a recurring dream. In the dream Charles Bukowski’s face and consciousness are grafted onto one of those inflatable dancing men that flop around in the wind outside of car dealerships. For some reason he is always stationed at a skeevy BMW reseller in Glendale. The detail feels stupid and also very important.

The plan was to turn this into a real scene. Actual air dancers in a parking lot. Silicone masks with sagging skin and cigarette burn textures. A sound piece that layered spoken word with mechanical fan noise and freeway air. The audio here, Bukowskis Car, was supposed to be the first draft of a much bigger thing.

What exists instead

One composition. A spoken word track sitting on top of a rough sound bed. It describes the dream as it kept repeating. Bukowski as a tube of vinyl and air, thrashing in front of a used car lot. The piece never grew past this version. There are no masks. No live air dancers. Just this recording and a folder of notes that read more like a stress log than a production plan.

Why it stalled

  • The stress that was feeding the dream caught up with me.
  • The line between research and delusion started to blur.
  • Every step forward required more time, more money, and more emotional distance than I had.

What this fragment reveals

That sometimes the body is already doing the performance and the project just records it. That a dream can feel like found footage. That it is possible to plan silicone masks and rented air dancers and still only have a single audio file at the end. The museum keeps this draft because it shows the point where an idea peaks and then collapses under the weight of the life that produced it.

Listening

This track is the first and only pass. Spoken word over a composition built while the dream was still repeating. Think of it as a test signal from a project that never reached the stage.
