Help me find my next book. I enjoy: Loss of memory is only temporary by Johanna Kaplan: What really struck me about this collection, was how the vignettes wove a picture of Jewish New York around the 1970s, from the viewpoint of a girl/ young woman who by most exterior accounts would be considered a little off. I found myself resonating with the characters, while also being able to live vicariously through them and by extension my mother and grandmother. How to talk dirty and influence people by Lenny Bruce: I really enjoy his sense of humor, and found his recollection of his life absolutely fascinating. Reading it felt as though I was transported back to a world that I would have experienced if I had been born a couple decades earlier. Any thing by tuvia tenenbaum: I really like his writing style. To me it gives me the pleasure of reading a memoir, but teaches me like an ethnographic work would. I also really like how he infuses humor into everything he writes about, and how every experience is his own despite how much he explores beyond the realm of what is his own. Carsick by John waters: first of all I must mention that John waters as a person and artist has been greatly influential on me. I love his humor as well as his love for all things distinctly distasteful. There is something incredibly kitschy and very specific to the American experience that is exuded in everything he produces, which I find myself very drawn to even when I don‚Äôt necessarily identify with it myself. I also love how he splits this book into three parts, his perception of the worst case scenario, best case scenario, and the scenario that actually ends of taking place. and in doing so establishes himself as an unreliable narrator in the most compelling and genuine way possible. Ham on rye by Charles bukowski: I initially read this book because I wanted to read something I identified with on a geographic level. So often my home, Los Angeles, is portrayed as a center for glitz and glam, or as a city of transplants in search of an artificial reality. Bukowski portrays the raw reality of a childhood and adolescence lived in Los Angeles. At times it is gritty, and gross, but it reminds me of my childhood in a way I can‚Äôt quite understand. Unlike bukowskis fictionalized version of himself, Hank, I did not grow up with a spiteful cheating father, or a pushover mother, I did not go to school in my own neighborhood, and I was never kicked out of my home. My background is fundamentally very different from Hank‚Äôs, but I can‚Äôt help but identify him. Like him, I was raised and corrupted by Los Angeles. I know the basements and garages where we all had our first drinks. I know the cheap restaurants where you never ordered anything but still sat there because you had nowhere better to be. While reading this book I felt as though I had walked the same steps as Hank, because at the end of the day I have. What makes Sammy run by Budd Shulberg: in all honesty I was not completely sold on the plot when initially pitched to me as my next read. I have heard a million stories about Hollywood as a land of vultures and over confident scam artists, and all of them have the same feeling. Not this one. This story did not aim to tell us what is happening but rather why it‚Äôs happening. I thoroughly enjoyed the metaphor that suggested that old Hollywood was a 20th century version of the gold rush, because even in the 21st century that idea still resonates. Overall it checked most of my boxes, it was Jewish, set for the most part in la, and had historical elements. Permanent midnight by Jerry Stahl: as a recovering addict who spent majority of their time actively addicted in Los Angeles this book hit me very close to home. Not only do I know many of the places stahl has used at, I myself have used at those places as well. I too balanced two worlds, but rather than spending my days in a writers room, I spent mine in a classroom at a private episcopal school in studio city. I had those same feelings that I wouldn‚Äôt be able to produce the work that I had to without using. For me this was a very cathartic read. Hitler in Los Angeles by Steve Ross: a compelling history of the nazi‚Äôs attempt at seizing the cultural zeitgeist of the United States through the ports of Los Angeles in the 1930s and 40s, and the Jewish man who set up an espionage network to take them down. Absolutely astounding book, uncovering the false narrative of American saviorism during the Second World War. Bad city: peril and power in the city of angels by Paul Pringle‚Äî this book follows La times journalist as he seeks to uncover the wrongdoings of usc keck school dean and ends up uncovering a web of corruption around Los Angeles all trying back to usc. In general I am absolutely smitten with nonfiction books about LA, and this book is no different. I love an honest book that seeks the truth no matter how difficult. This book not only seeks the truth but publishes it despite fighting an uphill battle against the beuroceacy that is Los Angeles.
