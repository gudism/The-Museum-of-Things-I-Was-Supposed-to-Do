figures.glb

What it was supposed to be

I took these 3D scans without a finished project in mind. The only real intention was to grab the people around me and run their faces through a machine. Not in a cute photogrammetry way. More like: take somebody’s likeness, peel it off as geometry, and see what breaks when you start pulling.

What exists instead

Three bodies in limbo: paul.glb, lucas.glb, kai.glb. They load. They spin. They sit on my desktop like evidence for a project that never fully admitted what it wanted to do to them. Every file is a person rendered as a lightweight asset waiting to be stretched, glitched, or dropped into a scene that still does not exist.

Why it stalled

  • The ethics and the impulse collided and neither one really won.
  • I wanted to distort the people I care about without fully deciding how cruel or tender that distortion should be.
  • No virtual space felt right for them to live in permanently, so they stayed homeless on my drive.

What this fragment reveals

That you can turn your friends into files faster than you can decide what it means. That a scan is not neutral just because it is accurate. That there are whole projects made of stalled intentions: half technical, half emotional, all sitting in a folder called something like figures while everyone goes on with their actual lives.
