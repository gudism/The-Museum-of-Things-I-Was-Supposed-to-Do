What it was supposed to be

A whole installation built from this one recurring dream I couldnâ€™t shake. Everytime he finds himself posted up in front of greasy Glendale car dealerships, as though it were his forever resting place. It sounds ridiculous, but the ridiculousness is the part that feels important.

The plan was to turn this into a real scene. Actual air dancers in a parking lot. Silicone masks with sagging skin and cigarette burn textures. The smell of cheap booze wafting from the inflatable man. A sound piece that layered spoken word with mechanical fan noise and freeway air. The audio here, Bukowskis Car, was supposed to be the first draft of a much bigger thing.

What exists instead

One composition. A spoken word track on top of a rough sound bed. It describes the dream as it kept repeating. Bukowski as a tube of vinyl and air, thrashing in front of a used car lot. The piece never grew past this version. No masks. No live air dancers. Just this recording and a folder of notes that read more like a stress log than a production plan.

Why it stalled

  • The stress that was feeding the dream caught up with me.
  • The line between research and delusion started to blur.
  • Every step forward required more time, more money, and more emotional distance than I had.

What this fragment reveals

That sometimes the body is already doing the performance and the project just documents its collapse. That a dream can feel like found footage. That you can plan silicone masks and rented air dancers and still only have one audio file at the end. The museum keeps this because it shows the moment when an idea peaks and then buckles under the weight of the life that produced it.
